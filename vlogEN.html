Our Vlogs

We document each day of our workshop in the videos below.

Today's lecture provided a comprehensive introduction to the FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC), including its timeline, rules, and the values it promotes. We explored the various awards and recognitions within the FRC community, emphasizing the importance of Gracious Professionalism. The discussion highlighted the skills developed through FRC participation and the essential skills required for success. Inspirational messages and safety practices were also covered, alongside useful resources for team management and technical guidance. Lastly, we delved into the design aspects of FRC robots, focusing on chassis and wheel types, and driving systems. A hands-on activity included building and testing four swerve drive wheels on robot, offering practical insights into advanced drive systems. We also have a chanse to drive and feel the outcome of swerve drive by driving a chassis from last year. What a lovely day to kick off a FRC team.

During this morning's session, we first learned about the build cycle of an FRC team during the regular season. Within a compact 6-week timeframe, a team needs to complete the following steps: analyzing the season's rules, proposing initial concepts and prototypes, designing the robot, building, programming, and iterating. This differs significantly from the competitions we have participated in before. Due to the tight build cycle, we cannot complete multiple iterations and tests. It is more important to complete and implement the concept proposed at the beginning of the season rather than completely borrowing the efficient robot designs of other teams.

Completing an FRC robot within six weeks requires efficient management and personnel allocation, mainly divided into two major categories of work: robot-related departments and logistics departments. The robot-related departments encompass all tasks from hardware to software, such as design, mechanics, electrical, programming, and operation. Collaboration among these departments is essential to complete the robot. Compared to FTC robots, FRC robots are more complex and require more engineering work. We need to pay attention to the details of each part, as any error could lead to accidents on the field. Additionally, since the time for repairs on the field is very tight, we need to ensure that the robot is nearly perfect before the competition.

Of course, an FRC team is not just composed of these technical departments; the logistics team is also crucial. The logistics team includes business/communication, audiovisual, outreach, and strategy/scouting. Although these logistics team members seem unrelated to the robot, it does not mean they are not important. On the contrary, an efficient logistics team can greatly help the team. Therefore, a well-established FRC team has nearly 40 members, each contributing their strengths.

In the afternoon, we continued the work on yesterday's chassis assembly. First, we added rubber to the four wheels and installed them on the swerve modules assembled yesterday. Today, our team members also entered the workshop and participated in the manufacturing process. During the fabricating process, we learned about the operation of the processing machines and the safety measures that need to be observed. After fully understanding the fabricating process, our team members also operated the machines themselves. We spray painted the fabricated parts black and red to enhance the robot's appearance. Finally, we assembled the chassis frame, installed the four swerve wheel modules, and completed the chassis assembly.

In the morning, in class, we learnt about the application and steps of CAD, CAM, and machine tools. We learned how to use CAM to manufacture various parts we need through the use of machinery after the design is completed in CAD. The teacher also took us step by step to understand how CAM works and its basic functions. This exposed us to the widely unseen side of robot construction. Usually, we would order materials from suppliers, but this time the teacher led us to understand how to make the parts needed for our robots on our own. Compared to before, we not only need to design on CAD but also program it on CAM in order for the machines we utilize to understand. Although it is more cumbersome, self-production can make us faster and more convenient in making our robots, as there is no longer a waiting period for the parts to arrive if we were to order it online, allowing us to finish the robot within these short days. The reason behind why we are learning these is to let us deeply understand the steps behind the manufacture of robot parts so that we can process our robots faster and better without the need of external aid. Afterward, when the teachers are processing other materials, we all had the opportunity to observe up close how these machines made our parts step by step from raw materials. There are both machine tools and CNC machining machines for us to see.

At noon, we started the turret test to see how the turret works under 12V voltage. This is because in the competition, we will use a computer program to limit its speed instead of 100% of it's speed to maintain a stable launch. We also learned how different materials, roller sizes, and roller spacing affect the launch of the notes. This can help us better understand its strengths and weaknesses, as well as its limits, helping us in the subsequent practice to aid us operate our robots and prevent problems with the launching device. In this process, some of us were responsible for adjustjng the launching turret, some were responsible for placing the notes, and others were specifically responsible for passing back the notes that were shot out.

After completing the test, in the afternoon, we started building our official turret. Team members divided the work to complete their respective tasks. Some prepared the PC board, installed sensors and lubricating tape on it, some made support bars, and others prepared the outer board, installing the motor. Everyone was fully focused on completing their part, and quickly completed the construction of the launching platform.

Today's course is a deep practical journey, which has given us a comprehensive understanding and understanding of the collection system of machines.

In the morning, we started with analyzing the collection requirements, and gradually deepened to every detail of the design. In the design process, we particularly emphasized the friction of the rollers, which is the key to ensuring effective collection and transportation. The rollers we designed not only need to have sufficient friction, but also need to have enough friction with the gun turret. The perfect docking of the transmitter requires a strong wheel set to provide the required driving force.

In the machining process, we applied the machining techniques we had previously learned and machined the required components. This process not only exercised our kinetic energy, but also deepened our understanding of mechanical design and machining. In practical operation, we encountered some challenges, such as difficulty in operating plastic pipe sleeves with silicon sleeves due to high friction. We cleverly used detergent as a lubricant, which not only solved the problem but also taught us the importance of being flexible and adaptable in practical operations.

In the afternoon construction task, we divided into groups and each person focused on completing their own part. We focused on learning the use of bearings and threads, and the correct installation and use of these key components are crucial for ensuring the stability and efficiency of the collection system. Through practical operations, we have mastered these skills and gained a deeper understanding of mechanical design and processing.

Through today's learning, we have realized the importance of combining theory and practice, and recognized the role of teamwork in problem-solving. We look forward to applying these new skills to competitions and daily life, and facing challenges.
